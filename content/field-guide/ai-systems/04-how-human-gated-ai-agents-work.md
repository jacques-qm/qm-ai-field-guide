The machine is not the interesting part of an agent. The gate is — the point where the work pauses for you to approve it before anything actually happens. Under the novelty, an agent is a short repeating loop with a checkpoint you own. Here is how one turn of it goes.

## Receive a goal

Work reaches the agent the way it already reaches you: an email arrives, a message comes in, a schedule fires. You don't build new habits around it. It sits behind the channels you already use.

## Think, plan, use tools

The agent reads the request, gathers the context it needs, and works out the steps. Then it acts on the systems you connected it to, and only those, with only the permissions you granted. Its reach is exactly what you handed it.

## Draft, and stop

It prepares the finished thing — a reply, a document, a calendar entry — and then it stops. Nothing has left your organization. The draft sits in a queue and you get a notification.

## The human gate

You read what it produced and decide: approve, edit and approve, or reject. Judging a finished draft takes seconds; producing it took the agent far longer, which is the trade you want. Your name stays on the result. As you build trust you can pre-approve routine, low-risk actions, but anything that leaves the organization still comes through you. That is a policy you set on purpose, not a default someone forgot to change.

## Execute, and remember

Only after you approve does the agent send, file, or update. Every action is written to a log. Then it records what happened — what you approved, what you changed — and your edits become standing instructions for next time. This is not the model retraining itself. It is the harness keeping notes, so the next task starts with your corrections already in hand.
