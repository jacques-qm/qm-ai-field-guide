You can pay for a flawless analysis of your problem and watch nothing change in the months that follow. The analysis isn't wrong. It just was never the part that was going to be hard.

## The deck that changes nothing

A good recommendation tells you what to do. It doesn't do it. The difficult, expensive, unglamorous work — building the thing inside your real systems and getting it to run reliably — still sits in front of you after the engagement ends, untouched. So the deck goes in a drawer and the problem stays exactly where it was.

## Where the work actually is

The difficulty was never in deciding what should exist. It's in the specifics: the data messier than anyone admitted, the in-house tool with no documentation, the edge case that only appears on the last day of the quarter. A recommendation can glide over all of that. Working software has to get through every bit of it before a single person can use it.

## The cost of every handoff

Each time the work changes hands, some understanding leaks out. Strategy gets compressed into a spec, the spec gets interpreted by builders, the builders ship to a team that runs it. By the end, the person who grasped the problem is several steps from the person fixing it, and the fix shows the distance. Keeping the understanding and the building in the same hands is how you stop paying that tax.

## Buy the result

Ask to pay for the thing working in production, not the plan for making it work. That is harder to deliver and harder to fake. It is also the only version that changes anything.
