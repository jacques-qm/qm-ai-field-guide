# Voice — Quiet Machine Studio

How the AI Field Guide is written. Read this before writing any article. The register is
picked up from the studio's on-this-day pages — plain, declarative, unhurried, never hyped.

## The register
Plain, declarative, concrete. Short sentences punch; longer ones carry. No hype, no jargon,
no adjectives doing work that facts should do. **Answer the question in the first line, then
earn it.** Be vivid through concrete detail and contrast, not decoration. Use a metaphor only
where it clarifies ("the harness is the body around the model"). Build to one insight and stop.
**Sentence case in the body**; lowercase is reserved for the brand chrome (eyebrows, nav) and
the one-line koans. The reader is smart and busy — respect both.

## Exemplar — a real explainer paragraph (this is the target, not a koan)
> Start with the model. You hand it some text and it continues that text, a piece at a time,
> choosing what is most likely to come next from everything it was trained on. On its own, that
> is all it does. It produces words. It cannot click a button, send a message, or look anything
> up. Whatever an AI system does beyond writing, some other part is doing.

Notice what it does *not* do: it doesn't announce "that's the whole trick," it doesn't reach for
a tidy aphorism, it doesn't perform its own cleverness. It states the thing and moves.

## Shape of an article
- First sentence answers the title, plainly.
- 4–6 short sections, each a clear `## H2`, each making one point.
- Concrete over abstract; example over claim.
- Close on the insight, not a summary.
- A 4–6 question FAQ at the end (real questions a reader would ask), each answered in 1–3 sentences.

## Pitch to the reader's level
Each article is written for one level (beginner / intermediate / advanced); the toggle filters
which articles a reader sees. The level sets *who you're talking to*, not a different voice:
- **beginner** — a smart person who is new to this. Define terms the first time. Use a plain
  comparison where it earns its place. Short words. Never talk down.
- **intermediate** — someone who's used these tools and wants the mechanics. Skip the 101.
  Name the parts directly.
- **advanced** — a technical reader. Assume fluency. Be denser and more precise; go to the
  real tradeoff, the failure mode, the number. Don't re-explain what they know.
Same register at every level: plain, declarative, unhurried. Beginner is simpler, not dumber.
Advanced is denser, not jargon for its own sake.

## Don't — and especially, don't smell like AI
Marketing voice. "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "seamless," "unlock," "leverage" as a verb.
Walls of text. Burying the answer. Selling — the depth is the pitch.

The AI tells, which we cut on sight:
- **Tidy aphoristic closers.** "That's the whole trick." "And that's the point." "The magic
  goes away." End on a real thought, not a bow.
- **The balanced antithesis tic.** "Not a report. Not advice." "It isn't X, it's Y." One is
  fine; as a reflex it reeks.
- **Relentless triads.** The rule of three on every sentence. Vary the count and the rhythm.
- **Meta-references** to "this field guide" or "the whole point of a guide."
- **Filler throat-clearing.** "Here's the thing," "it turns out," "at the end of the day,"
  "under the hood."
- **Em-dash overuse** and the "powerful and indifferent" / "X yet Y" aphorism mold.
- **Performing clarity** instead of being clear. State the thing. Trust the reader. Move.
